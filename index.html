Editor's Note


Katie Cartee


Seed has grown so much, I almost can’t believe that we started out with less than ten people and no class period just a couple of short years ago. It’s absolutely amazing how far we’ve come. I feel like each issue gets better and better. This semester alone we’ve had so many new ideas and people. Christopher’s website is new and up and running, meaning that if you want to view the magazine with uninterrupted scrolling and a wonderful layout, you can read it there. I’d like to welcome and thank all our new staff members, for learning so quickly and working hard to make this issue the best we could. I don’t think I’ve ever worked with a group of people, both new and those who are returning from last year, who work so well together. I’d go crazy without them. I think this was the smoothest magazine-creation process we’ve had. This issue happened without any major problems, mostly because of that harmony. Crunch week is always tough but they handled it like pros and deserve all the thanks you and I can give them.

I’d like to talk about the people who submitted things, too. I know I’m at risk of sounding sappy here, but I appreciate every single piece we get. Keep ‘em coming, please! Everything you send is enjoyed and discussed by the Seed staff. Also, a side note: if you don’t see a piece of your work in this issue, but you did submit something, it’s not because we hated it or you. We’ve already begun stockpiling things for spring and you’ll probably see it in the next issue.

Enjoy the magazine and I hope to see you all in spring!

Today dawned purple,
a miracle of light
and cloud. Were I blessed with
the hand of a great artist,

and spread the sky on paper,

A Night on the Town


Nicholas Gehrdes


Even on the darkest of nights, life and light emanated from the City. The streets bustled with activity and people, surging like a tide. Above their heads stood great towers, tall and strong, monuments of days long gone. The City was home to many people in the Dead World, from Elves who served in the Great War to Lycanthropes just trying to scrape by. Beside them were both Silvs on their horses, watching the crowd diligently as cars whizzed by them.

Looking down upon this surging tide was a dwarf in his office, his thick muscular arms, barely restrained in his button up shirt, folded across his broad chest. The office he was in was dimly lit at the moment, the only light coming from the streets below. He breathed silently, thinking. His attention was snapped when he heard the door behind him open. He turned his head to look.

In the doorway was a tall, muscular woman with black hair and almond colored skin. She leaned against the doorway, her form lit up from the light below.

"What are you doing here so late?" asked the Dwarf, his hard gaze returning to the crowd.

"I'd ask the same of you," the woman replied, stepping into the room.

"I've been busy."

"Staring out of windows?"

"It's hard work."

"I can tell," the woman said.

"Is there something you want?" the Dwarf asked, looking up at his assistant. She was surprisingly well dressed. Normally she wore a white button up shirt and a black trench coat, a long sword attached to her hip. Tonight, however, she wore an elegant, black backless dress. Her short, raven hair was swept back. Around her neck hung a pendant of Mjolnir, "It's not my money, is it?"

She scoffed, "Do you really think so low of me? No, I wanted to see if you wanted to have a night out, you boring sod."

The man was quiet for a moment, before letting off a shrug, "I don't see why not. What did you have in mind?"

The lady pondered for a moment, folding her arms across her chest. While she was thinking, the Dwarf grabbed his coat and brought it over his shoulders, shoving his arms through the sleeves.

“We could always go to that one place that’s only a few blocks away,” She offered. “My friends tell me it’s really good.”

“You have friends?”

“More than you.”

“I’d hardly call that a feat.” The Dwarf said smugly. “What’s the place called?”

“Above the Cut.” She said, walking ahead of the Dwarf and outside the door. “Try to keep up with those stubby legs of yours.”

“You stay all night thinking that one up?”

“Half of it. The other half was trying to think of a reason why the chicken crossed the road.”

The Dwarf snorted in amusement, pressing the down button on the elevator. The elevator chimed once it reached the floor, its doors sliding open quickly. They entered quietly, having run out of snarky things to say to each other (for now anyway). His assistant pressed the button to the lobby.

“So what all do they serve at this place?” the bearded dwarf asked, twiddling with his beard. The woman in black shrugged. The Dwarf had just noticed that she had shining red lipstick and black mascara. For as long as he knew her, she never wore make up.

“As far as I know they serve really good cuts of meat, hence the name.” she responded, looking down at her boss. “My friends say that the ettin is quite good, same with the mutton.”

The Dwarf grunted in response. At times it was hard to tell whether he was approving of something or just showing that he understood the message. The elevator dinged again, the doors slinging open with a loud chunk sound.

They exited the building, trying to hail a taxi. It felt like an eternity before a yellow car rolled up to them. They entered the car, the woman telling the driver, a man who’s features the Dwarf couldn’t make out through all the smoke and the relative darkness of the taxi, of their destination.

“Where to?” he asked. His voice was gravelly, as if he had just eaten a bucket of nails.

“Above the Cut, please.” the woman said.

The Taxi driver nodded silently, his gaze returning to the street. He drove to the destination in relative silence. The Dwarf looked outside the window with his fist under his chin. He was deep in thought. The only thing that has been on his mind was the murder. He was more than happy to go out tonight and have some fun, to finally get his mind off of this case. He closed his eyes, just to sleep for a bit.

They were seated quickly and promptly had their drinks served. They talked quietly, telling the other about what they found about the case. They both came to the conclusion of “we didn’t find much”. The Dwarf ordered some ettin, medium-rare of course, and the Woman ordered mutton. Both of them were seasoned well, with the troll meat having a nice light purple center.

The Dwarf pressed his palms together and the Woman bowed her head, giving thanks to their separate Gods before eating. They began eating after prayers. The meat tasted as good as it looked and the bread served with it was warm and crunchy. There was no greenery on the plate. Winter was harsh and there was not enough land within walls for green food. The bread is easy enough to trade and most meats could either be hunted or raised.

“Thank you.” The Dwarf said.

“For what?”

“For taking me out. It helps take my mind off things.”

“Don’t thank me yet.” Hildr said with a sly grin, her white teeth shining through her red lips. “I’m not doing this for free.”

“Is that a fact?”

“Oh yes. You’ll see how later.”

Beneath the Snow


Jasmine Willis

His heart lies
Beneath the snow,

Wet and cold
From tears of woe.

His wife dies
And he screams no,

But she is sucked
Beneath the snow.

He begged the earth
To let her go,

But there's no escape
Beneath the snow.

That same night
Continues his plight.

His son's fever was rising high;
The hour of his knell would soon be nigh.

A final breath
Carried fateful death.

Father dressed son in suit and bow
And tucked him in beneath the snow.

He would pay any cost
To regain what he lost.

He wanted revenge on the thief
Who stole his joy and left him grief.

But time passed on;
His passion was gone.

That, too, seeped down
Beneath the snow.

All he had seemed to flow
Into that grave beneath the snow.

Joys and sorrows,
Yesterdays and tomorrows,

His whole vitality
And personality,

Even sanity
And humanity,

Lept from his soul
Down into the hole.

It swallowed time and space
And the entirety of his race.

At last everything was gone;
He knew he could not live on.

He lay down, let go,
And lost everything

Beneath the snow.

Patient 93


J.T. Cook


"It does not appear the patient is in a comatose state as of now. For a while I was worried he would not make it after he came in." The gruff voice takes a breath as if he is waiting for something. The patient hears papers shuffling and a pencil scratching against a hard surface. "The results from his blood work—the serum creatine phosphokinase and serum cerculoplasmin levels—are extremely high." The voice, deep and harsh, pauses for a second before continuing, "With his recent behavior compounded with the results of these tests, I say there is a high probablitiy he has schizophrenia."

"So what do you want to do with the patient, Doctor Iscariot?" The response comes in a whisper, soft and gentle compared to the gruff, cruel voice of the man. The patient tries to open his eyes, trying to glimpse at the woman, but they refuse to open. Hopefully, she looks as wonderful as her melodious voice. The Doctor Iscariot's silence fills the room. Papers rustle to the left of his head. Then, the doctor heaves a sigh, filling the deaf room with sound. Silence ensues again. More rustling papers. "Schizophrenia," he trails off again. "So with his recent offense, he will have to be transported to a different hospital. There, we can keep a closer eye on him before prescribing medication and treaments."

"Yes, Doctor Iscariot. I will make the necessary arrangements for his transportation immediately."

"Thank you, Eva." After the cut response, he hears a pair of feet shuffling out of the room. The door closes with a clink behind the person, leaving the other one in the room with him. He wonders who is waiting beside him. It is impossible by just the breathing.

When the person starts to hum, he affirms it is the nurse, Eva. Her pen scratches against paper in a hurried manner. A short pause in writing as the papers flip before writing starts again. After a long while, the pen stops its incessant scratching and she clips the papers to something. The sounds of her feet circle around him before stopping by his life side.

"Can you hear me?" Eva whispers into his ear. He tries to respond, but nothing is able to come out. He assumes the staff or doctors must have drugged him upon his arrival on the hospital. Trying to talk is most definitely out. Instead, he tries to move a finger to respond; however, his effort is futile. He wonders how he can get her to understand he could hear her, but his mind keeps drawing a blank. But that did not stop her from going on to whisper back at him in a hurried manner.

"This place is going to be a nightmare for you. Don't ever lose youself because if and when you do, you will be a murderer." A pause before her voice, thick with sweetness, murmurs, "But you are already one. Aren't you?"


Daniel Chen

We Stand United, We Fall Together


Allison Sabourin


The magic churned inside me, and I felt any control I had over it slipping away. My body burned with pain, and I struggled, trying to get away from it, but it was inside of me. There was nothing I could do.

I screamed as darkness danced up my arms; it felt like it was burning me. The empire was torturing me for my disobedience, but I would never give in to them again. I would be betraying everyone.

I had to be strong for them so I could escape.

If this magic didn’t kill me first, I would not allow myself to be near anyone I cared about. It was unpredictable and dangerous; I would not put them in danger anymore.

The pressure was building again; my body couldn’t handle this magic. I screamed in pain, rage, and defiance as the wave of darkness escaped my body again. The force of it snapped some of my chains, releasing my hands. I slumped to the ground, not even trying to hold myself up anymore; the exhaustion was too much. I closed my eyes and fell asleep instantly.

The days blurred as my body grew stronger from repeated release of the magic. The pressure on my body lessened every day I stayed in the cell.

I didn’t know how much time passed before I saw another human being. The surges of power had all but stopped, and I could feel that I was stronger. A soldier entered my cell one day and appraised me coolly. At first glance, he looked to be nothing special. He was scrawny, and I could tell he had been a coward at one time in his life. But upon closer inspection, his eyes were completely black and devoid of emotion. He could care less about anyone or anything. Fearfully, I wondered if he had been like me at one point.

“You’re joining the elite force tomorrow. The commanders believe that you are ready. I hope, for your sake, they are right. You leave tomorrow.”

I watched the soldier leave with a sinking feeling in my chest. What were they going to try to force me to do now?

I shook my head. I couldn’t think like that. I had to get out of the army, and this was my chance. I was determined to get out when they brought me to the elite force. Maybe these new found powers would be my escape.


The Toy Maker


Jasmine Willis


Innocence. Perfection. Virtue. These things I have devoted my life to finding and sheltering from the corrupting rain of humanity. Those that speak of me—only the frightened, close-minded rumors that are circulated throughout a society that simply will not understand, for none (alive) know me—call me the Toy Maker. I am perfect, or so I believe. I have neither name nor personality; I exist solely for the sake of humanity, to save that which is pure before the abyssal, ever-hungry pit of the sinful human soul gobbles down a second helping of the forbidden fruit. Despite the swift contamination of many of my subjects before I can save them, I perform my job well, and my creations are startlingly… lifelike.

My heroic rescue of the purity of mankind is an exact and delicate cycle, and having just completed my preservation of one of the more fortunate members of humankind, I return to the beginning of said cycle: locating a subject. Such is the reason why I walk the streets on this autumnal night graced by the algid presence of winter before its due time.

In the slosh of mud and trodden snow, I detect a small hint of a footprint, surely no larger than a child’s, or what would seem a child to me. A light snow begins to fall, and, before it smothers the tracks, I hurry in the path of the footsteps in search of the virtuous garden to which they undoubtedly lead. I am directed into a park, a quaint, little one on the poorer side of the city. What sight the footprints guide me to lay eyes upon—well, it is times like these that I think human goodness may yet be saved from that all-consuming flame. My target sits beneath the cover of an evergreen tree, and I approach so that I may more effectively scrutinize the material. But what is this! Two snakes in the garden tormenting—tempting—my precious subject!

Two children in their ignorance, which is perhaps the most dangerous sin of humans, militantly accost my subject, smearing dirt onto her face—her perfect, angelic face!—and ripping at her hair. I, being of a more delicate, gentle nature, cannot watch such acts of savagery—nay, humanity, for they are one and the same—and step in...

Artist Spotlight: Daniel Chen


You may have noticed that Daniel Chen has a number of pieces of artwork being used in the magazine this issue. He’s a talented artist and the Seed staff loved basically every piece he gave us. Because of this and to thank him for being amazing, we have named Daniel Chen this issue’s featured artist. The following are a handful of questions he answered.

When did you start doing art?

I started doing art as a young child, but it was always just a weekend chore every Sunday. It was never such a big part of my life until now though.

Do you have a favorite or preferred medium?

I don’t really have a preferred medium, but Graphite Pencils are where I started and what I believe to be the most essential drawing medium to be able to use.

What is your favorite art museum? Who is your favorite artist?

Recently, I visited the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, and was captivated by all the phenomenal artwork by artists that I’ve only begun to study in books. It was an overwhelming experience that really opened my eyes into the world of art. The Museum of Modern Art in New York was definitely a memorable experience because all the interesting and controversial artwork inside. In terms of a favorite artist, not very many come to mind. However, I do appreciate the style of impressionism immensely.

Where do you draw your inspiration from?

Matthew Tiller has been a big part in my life. He inspired me on the soccer pitch and in the classroom, you know? At its core, all my art is drawn from Mathew Tiller, the BIG MAN.

Do you listen to music while creating your art? If so, what’s going on your playlist? Or do you have another habit while working?

Since it’s the holiday season, I just have Christmas music playing 24/7. Besides music, I don’t really have any habits while working.

What are your plans for the future?

For college, I was just recently accepted to the Rhode Island School of Design. In terms of careers, I plan to hopefully find a job in the design field for companies such as Apple or Google. The ultimate dream, however, is to become a professional artist.

Do you have other skills and hobbies in addition to art?

I really have a lot of hobbies from sports like table tennis and soccer ranging to fun things like yoyos and games. Science Olympiad Season is quickly approaching and I plan on building as many planes and bridges as I can.
